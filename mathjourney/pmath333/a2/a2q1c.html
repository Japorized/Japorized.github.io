--- layout: blog section-type: default_markdown title: A2Q1(c) --- # Statement Let $$ \langle a_k \rangle _{k\geq p} $$ and $$ \langle b_k \rangle _{k\geq p} $$ be sequences in $$ \mathbb{R} $$ with $$\lim_{k\to\infty} a_k = c $$ where $$0 < c \in \mathbb{R}$$ and $$\lim_{k\to\infty} b_k = \infty$$. Use the definition of the limit to show that $$\lim_{k\to\infty} \frac{a_k}{b_k} = 0$$. # Attempted Proof $$\lim_{k\to\infty} b_k = \infty \Leftrightarrow \forall \epsilon > 0, \exists m_1 \in \mathbb{Z}, \forall k \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq p}(k\geq m_1 \implies b_k \geq r)$$. $$\lim_{k\to\infty} a_k = c \Leftrightarrow \forall \epsilon > 0, \exists m_2 \in \mathbb{Z}, \forall k \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq p}(k\geq m_2 \implies \vert a_k - c \vert \leq r\epsilon - c)$$. Now $$ \forall \epsilon > 0$$, choose $$m \in \mathbb{Z}$$ such that $$m=max\{m_1,m_2\}$$. Then $$\forall k \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq p} \bigg(k \geq m \implies (\vert a_k - c \vert \leq r\epsilon - c \wedge b_k \geq r)\bigg)$$. Then $$ \begin{align*} \vert \frac{a_k}{b_k} \vert & = \frac{\vert a_k - c + c \vert}{\vert b_k \vert} \\ & \leq \frac{\vert a_k - c \vert}{\vert b_k \vert} + \frac{c}{\vert b_k \vert} \\ & \leq \frac{r\epsilon -c}{r} + \frac{c}{r} \\ & = \epsilon \end{align*} $$ Thus $$\lim_{k\to\infty} \frac{a_k}{b_k} = 0$$ as required.
